TURTLE HATCHERIES

Observe the threatened sea turtle, whose inquisitive behavior will captivate you beyond belief. Sea turtles are an endangered species in many parts of the world, and turtle hatcheries are essential to their preservation and protection. These facilities are devoted to protecting sea turtles and their natural habitats, with a particular emphasis on the critical first few months of a turtle's existence, from nesting through hatching.

SRI LANKA TURTLE POPULATION


Five of the seven species of sea turtles in the world visit the shores of Sri Lanka to breed. They are the Green Turtle (Chelonia mydas), Hawksbill Turtle (Eretmochelys imbricata), Olive Ridley (Lepidochelys olivacea), Loggerhead (Caretta caretta) and Leatherback (Dermochelys coriacea). The sea turtles are called Kesbawa in Sinhala and Amai in Tamil. There are three species of freshwater turtles and one terrestrial tortoise in Sri Lanka. The freshwater turtles are Parker's Black Turtle (Melanochelys trijuga pakeri) Sri Lanka Black Turtle and the Soft or Flapshell turtle. The terrestrial tortoise is the Star tortoise

Types Of Turtles Found

Green Turtle
The Green turtle (Chelonia mydas), one of the biggest marine turtles ever recorded, is the one that is most frequently spotted in Sri Lanka. It may be identified by its flattened body, which is encased in a tear-shaped shell that is blackish grey in color, a smaller head than the loggerhead turtle, and a pair of enormous flippers that resemble paddles. The average adult turtle weighs between 68 and 190 kg, however rare species can reach weights of 315 kg.

Loggerhead turtles
Loggerhead turtles (Caretta caretta) are magnificent sea turtles that inhabit the coastal waters of Sri Lanka. They are one of the most recognizable species of sea turtles, characterized by their large heads and powerful jaws. Loggerheads are known for their impressive size, with adults reaching an average length of around 3 feet (1 meter) and weighing between 200 and 350 pounds (90-160 kilograms).

Leatherback turtle
The leatherback turtle, scientifically known as Dermochelys coriacea, is a fascinating marine reptile that holds the distinction of being the largest turtle species in the world. These incredible creatures are found in various oceans around the globe, including the waters surrounding Sri Lanka, a beautiful island nation in South Asia.

Best Times To Visit Turtle Hacheries

The best time to visit turtle hatcheries in Sri Lanka is typically from May to September. During these months, many turtle hatcheries along the coast are actively involved in conservation efforts to protect turtle eggs and hatchlings. It's important to note that turtle hatcheries are open year-round, but the nesting season and hatchling releases are more common during this period. Visiting a turtle hatchery provides a unique opportunity to learn about the conservation efforts aimed at protecting these endangered species. You can witness the process of collecting turtle eggs, caring for the hatchlings, and releasing them into the ocean. Hatcheries often provide educational programs and informative displays to raise awareness about sea turtle conservation.

What Is a Turtle Hatchery?


A turtle hatchery is a facility dedicated to protecting and conserving sea turtles by providing a controlled environment for the incubation and hatching of turtle eggs. These hatcheries play a crucial role in the conservation of sea turtle populations, especially in areas where turtle nesting sites are threatened by human activity, predation, or other natural hazards. The process at a turtle hatchery typically involves the following steps: 1. Nesting: Female sea turtles come ashore to lay their eggs in the sand. Hatcheries often work in collaboration with local communities and conservation organizations to identify and protect nesting sites. 2.Egg collection: Once a nesting sea turtle lays her eggs, the eggs may be carefully collected by trained personnel to be moved to a safer location, such as the hatchery. This relocation helps protect the eggs from predators, poachers, or natural disturbances. 3.Incubation: At the hatchery, the collected turtle eggs are placed in artificial nests, typically in specially designed incubation chambers.
